About Karachi

Karachi is the beating economic heart of Pakistan and its largest city by both population and geographical area. Situated along the northern coast of the Arabian Sea in Sindh province, the city serves as the country's principal seaport and commercial capital, responsible for the majority of Pakistan's GDP, trade, and industrial output. Yet beyond its economic significance, Karachi is a city of extraordinary cultural diversity – home to communities from every corner of Pakistan and a vibrant mix of Sindhi, Muhajir, Balochi, Pashtun, and Punjabi cultures.

Travellers drawn to Karachi encounter a city of contrasts: colonial-era architecture standing alongside modern high-rises, traditional bazaars buzzing metres from international shopping malls, and serene beachside sunsets within a sprawling megacity. The city's turbulent but rich history, running from its origins as a small fishing village through British colonial rule to its role as the first capital of independent Pakistan, has bequeathed it a uniquely layered character found nowhere else in the country.

Top Attractions

Clifton Beach (Sea View)

Clifton Beach, popularly known as Sea View, is Karachi's most famous and frequented coastal destination. Stretching along the Arabian Sea coastline in the upscale Clifton district, the beach extends for several kilometres and offers sweeping views of the open sea, particularly spectacular at sunset when the horizon blazes in shades of orange and crimson. The beach is a social institution in Karachi, where families, couples, and vendors converge each evening in a lively, uniquely Karachi scene.

The beach promenade is lined with food stalls serving Karachi's beloved street food – grilled corn, bun kebabs, chaat, and fresh coconut water. Horse and camel rides are popular with families, and the Clifton Urban Forest, a restored green space developed in recent years, provides a peaceful escape from the concrete city. Boat launches operate from the beach for short sea excursions, and on public holidays the atmosphere takes on a festive, carnival-like energy that encapsulates the city's spirit perfectly.

Visitor Info

Clifton Beach is accessible year-round and is free to visit. The beach is busiest from late afternoon to around 9 PM. The best time to visit is during the cooler months (October to February) when the sea breeze is pleasant. Public transport options include buses, Careem, and Uber.

Mohatta Palace

Mohatta Palace is one of Karachi's finest examples of Indo-Saracenic architecture and an emblem of the city's layered colonial past. The palace was originally built in 1927 as a summer retreat for Seth Shivratan Mohatta, a wealthy Hindu Marwari businessman, under the design supervision of architect Agha Ahmed Hussain. The building is distinctive for its pink Jodhpur sandstone and yellow Gizri stone facade, giving it a rich, warm appearance that has made it a celebrated city landmark.

After the partition of British India in 1947, the palace was occupied by the Fatima Jinnah, the sister of Pakistan's founding father, Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah. Following her death in 1967, the property passed through several governmental uses before being restored and converted into a museum in 1999. Today, Mohatta Palace Museum hosts permanent and rotating exhibitions covering Pakistani art, craft, and cultural history. The surrounding gardens and ornamental exterior details make the palace one of the most photographed buildings in Karachi.

Visitor Info

Open Tuesday to Sunday, 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM (closed Mondays). A modest entry fee applies. Photography is permitted in the exterior gardens; restrictions apply in some interior galleries.

Pakistan Maritime Museum

The Pakistan Maritime Museum, situated in the Clifton-Saddar area near Karachi's historic harbour, is a unique and rewarding institution dedicated to the naval and maritime heritage of Pakistan. Established in 1988 and operated by the Pakistan Navy, the museum's outdoor precinct features an impressive array of decommissioned naval vessels and military hardware – the centrepiece being PNS Babur, a retired Pakistani Navy destroyer that visitors can board and explore below deck.

The indoor galleries trace the history of maritime trade in the subcontinent from ancient Indus Valley civilisations through the Arab dhow traders, the colonial era, and the formation of the Pakistan Navy in 1947. Exhibits include navigational instruments, scale models of historical and modern warships, armaments, uniforms, and rare archival photographs. There is also a dedicated submarine section showcasing the PNS Hangor – the submarine that sank an Indian frigate during the 1971 war, marking the first submarine combat kill in Asia since World War II.

Visitor Info

Open Tuesday to Sunday, 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM. Nominal entry fee. Visitors are advised to bring a valid ID. The outdoor exhibits are the highlight – allow at least 2–3 hours for a thorough visit.
