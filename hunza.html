Hunza Valley – Heaven on Earth

A jewel of the Karakoram, Hunza Valley stuns visitors with its turquoise lakes, glacier-carved peaks, ancient forts, and a resilient culture born at the roof of the world.

About Hunza Valley

Nestled in the Gilgit-Baltistan region at altitudes exceeding 2,400 metres, Hunza Valley is one of Pakistan's most beloved mountain destinations. Flanked by some of the world's highest peaks – Rakaposhi, Ultar Sar, and Lady Finger – the valley stretches along the Hunza River and is traversed by the legendary Karakoram Highway (KKH), which connects Pakistan to China through some of the most dramatic high-altitude terrain on the planet.

The people of Hunza, the Burusho, are celebrated for their warm hospitality, the rich Burushaski language unique to the region, and a centuries-old tradition of apricot cultivation. Each spring, the valley explodes in a blanket of pink and white blossom as thousands of apricot trees flower simultaneously – a spectacle that draws photographers from around the globe. Hunza is a destination that rewards the adventurous traveller with memories that last a lifetime.

Top Attractions

Rakaposhi Viewpoint

Rising to a commanding height of 7,788 metres, Rakaposhi is the 27th highest peak in the world and one of the most visually dominant mountains on Earth. Unlike many Karakoram giants that are obscured by surrounding ridges, Rakaposhi rises almost directly from the valley floor, offering a vertical relief of nearly 6,000 metres – one of the greatest uninterrupted ascents on the planet.

The most popular and accessible vantage point is the Rakaposhi Viewpoint at Diran Base Camp near Minapin village, reachable by a moderate two-hour trek. On clear mornings, the mountain's permanent snow and ice fields gleam a brilliant white while the lower slopes display alpine meadows carpeted with wildflowers. Many visitors choose to linger here for several hours to witness the mountain transform in colour through the changing light of the day.

Trekking Tip

The trail to Minapin Base Camp starts at Minapin village, approximately 43 km from Karimabad. The two-hour uphill hike is of moderate difficulty and is best undertaken with a local guide. Carry water and wear layered clothing as temperatures drop sharply at altitude.

Attabad Lake

Attabad Lake is a breathtaking high-altitude reservoir with an extraordinary origin story. In January 2010, a massive landslide near the village of Attabad dammed the Hunza River, creating a natural lake nearly 22 kilometres long over a period of five months. The catastrophic event displaced over 6,000 people and submerged the original Karakoram Highway for several years, yet in its aftermath created one of Pakistan's most photogenic landscapes.

The lake's water, fed by glacial meltwater, is a vivid, almost supernatural shade of turquoise – a colour that photographers struggle to capture faithfully. Boat rides across the lake offer jaw-dropping panoramas of sheer cliffs plunging into the water and snow-capped peaks rising behind them. Several guesthouses and cafés have been established on the lakeshore, making Attabad a perfect overnight stop on the KKH journey north.

Visitor Info

Attabad Lake is located approximately 8 km from Aliabad on the Karakoram Highway. Boat rides are available from the lakeside jetties and are reasonably priced. The lake is accessible year-round, but summer (May–September) offers the best weather and most vibrant water colour.

Baltit Fort

Perched dramatically above the town of Karimabad on a rocky promontory, Baltit Fort commands a sweeping view of the entire Hunza Valley and the surrounding Karakoram peaks. The fort is believed to be over 700 years old, with its origins traced to a Tibetan princess who brought craftsmen from Baltistan to build her a palace-fortress when she married the Mir (ruler) of Hunza. Over the centuries, each ruling Mir added wings, watchtowers, and ceremonial halls, resulting in a structure that blends Tibetan, Kashmiri, and Central Asian architectural styles.

Baltit Fort served as the royal residence of the Mirs of Hunza until 1945, when the last Mir relocated to a new palace. After decades of neglect, the Aga Khan Cultural Service Pakistan undertook a meticulous restoration programme in the 1990s that preserved the fort's original timber-and-stone construction techniques. Today it operates as a living museum, displaying the personal artefacts, weapons, royal garments, and household items of the Hunza royal family. The rooftop terrace offers arguably the finest mountain panorama in Gilgit-Baltistan.

Visitor Info

Open daily from 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM (closed during winter months – November to February). Entry fee applies. Guided tours in English are conducted by trained local guides and are highly recommended.
